# What is Judo

---

Judo, meaning "the gentle way" or "the way of giving way", is a Japanese martial art and sport created by Dr. Jigoro Kano in 1882. Kano refined the ancient fighting system of Jujutsu into a system based on two core principles:

1. **Maximum efficiency with minimum effort**
2. **Mutual welfare and benefit**

The first principle involves using technique and timing to throw an opponent while expending minimal physical strength. The second principle suggests that although Judo is physically demanding, applying its principles to everyday life enables individuals to translate physical concepts of maximum efficiency and "giving way" into mutual aid and concession in society, resulting in greater harmony among people.

By eliminating dangerous Jujutsu techniques such as kicking, punching, and small joint locks, Kano created a system where techniques could be practiced at full speed with minimal injury risk. The preserved and refined techniques include a large variety of throws and ground control methods such as pins, chokes, and arm locks.

Students first master "ukemi," the art of falling properly to avoid injury, enabling daily training without recovery time between sessions.

---

## As a Sport

Points in competitive judo are awarded for throwing or pinning an opponent. Alternatively, matches may be won by forcing an opponent to submit to a choke or arm lock. Judo became a sanctioned Olympic sport beginning with the 1964 Tokyo Olympics, with countless levels of competitions available for players of all experience levels.

Whether practiced for exercise, recreation, competition, self-defense, or as a life philosophy, Judo is an inexpensive activity enjoyed year-round by women and men, boys and girls of all ages. Dojos worldwide welcome players of all experience levels.
