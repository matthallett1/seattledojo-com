# Seattle Dojo - About / History

**North America's First Judo Dojo**

---

## Introduction

Seattle Dojo stands as the oldest judo facility in the United States, established in the early 1900s. The organization relocated multiple times before constructing its present building during the 1930s by first-generation Japanese Americans.

The founder of judo, Professor Jigoro Kano, made visits to the dojo in 1936 and 1938. The club organized its inaugural tournament in 1903 and sponsored numerous competitions between 1907-1941. Operations ceased during World War II but resumed in 1948, with the first post-war tournament held in 1953. The annual tournament has continued uninterrupted since that time.

## Training Programs

The facility offers instruction for participants across all age groups and experience levels under several qualified instructors. Sessions occur Monday and Thursday evenings, accommodating both fitness-focused participants and serious competitive judoka. Specialized youth classes teach fundamental techniques in an encouraging setting, helping children develop physical skills, self-assurance, discipline, and community engagement.

## Historical Background (from Wikipedia)

### Location and Structure

The dojo occupies a building at 1510 S. Washington in Seattle's Squire Park neighborhood. The oldest judo dojo in the continental United States was founded before 1907 in what is now the International District.

### Establishment History

On October 17, 1903, Kodokan leader Yoshitsugu Yamashita demonstrated judo at Seattle Theatre. The pioneer instructor was Itaro Kono, a 2-Dan black belt who arrived November 29, 1905. Tokugoro Ito, a professional wrestler, made the club his headquarters (1907-1911).

### Building Details

Built spring 1934 by architect Kichio Allen Arai, the structure features frame construction mounted on truck springs, literally providing the floor with spring action.

### Tournament History

The first regional tournament occurred March 1907. From 1909-1941, events typically occurred at Seattle's Nippon Kan Theatre. Washington teams competed against California counterparts in 1936, 1937, and 1939. Jigoro Kano was present at the 1936 Los Angeles tournament.

### Post-WWII Era

The dojo reopened January 1, 1947, following forced closure due to wartime restrictions and Japanese American internment. Key figures reopening the facility included Toru Araki, Akira "Poison" Kato, Hiromu "Kelly" Nishitani, and Dick Yamasaki.

The 1953 tournament was historic -- the first Pacific Northwest tournament in which women participated. Five women were involved, three from Portland, Oregon, and two from Vancouver, British Columbia.

During May 1954, a Seattle team won first place at the US National AAU judo championships, featuring Kenji Yamada, Shuzo "Chris" Kato, Charles Woo, Tats Kojima, and George Wilson.

### Notable Instructors and Students

**Instructors:** Iitaro Kono, Tokugoro Ito, R. Fukuda, Daisuke Sakai, Eitaro Suzuki, Masataro Shibata, Hideo Hama, Hiroshi Kurosaka, Yasuyuki Kumagai, Isamu "Sam" Furuta, Shuzo "Chris" Kato, Fred Sato, and Kenji Yamada.

**Prominent former students:** Professional wrestler Kaimon Kudo, Southern California judo leader Ken Kuniyuki, respected competitor Masato Tamura, martial arts historian Robert W. Smith, and Japanese American Citizens League pioneer James Y. Sakamoto.

### Associated Clubs

Related dojos developed across the region:
- Tacoma Dojo (1917)
- Fife Dojo (1923)
- Eatonville (1938)
- South Park (1924)
- White River (1927)
- Green Lake (1932)
- Bainbridge Island (1932)
- Yakima Valley (1935)

A rival organization, Tentokukan (1928), established competing clubs in O'Brien (1929), Sunnydale (1932), Bellevue (1932), and Spokane (1937).

### Prominent Visitors Pre-WWII

- Tsunejiro Tomita visited in 1910
- Hideichi Nagaoka arrived in 1934
- Jigoro Kano visited in 1932 and 1938
